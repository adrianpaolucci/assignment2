CO₂ Emissions over the Globe

Written by Adrian Matteo Paolucci

The change in environment over time across the globe has been well documented up until this point. It is widely known that statistics such as CO₂ emissions, forestation and global warming are some parameters which have made the world believe the environment is in trouble. Is this really the case? This page delves into the impact of forestation, trade activity and agriculture on CO₂ emissions and temperature change. We will also look into impact by country and continent and see if the same trends apply to respective continents.





Forestation and CO₂ Emissions


Forestation

Forestation is represented by the amount of forest land as a percentage of total land. This statistic may show correlation to CO₂ emissions as environmentally friendly countries are typically more 'green'.

Although percentage forestation is not the only factor influencing CO₂ emissions, the way that CO₂ is absorbed by greenery theoretically should reduce CO₂ emissions in the given country.

Geographical Relationship

From the map, we can see some continents such as South America and parts of Africa have more forestation compared to less 'green' areas.

The forestation number has a twofold infuence on a Country's environmental impact. Firstly, more greenery means the plantation can absorb more Carbon Dioxide from the atmosphere. Secondly, a country may care more about environmental issues by maintaining their forestation as a high percentage of their land.




CO₂ Emissions and GDP


GDP Per Capita

GDP is measured as 'GDP per capita', which means the Gross Domestic Product of a country is divided by the number of people residing in the country. GDP per capita is used in this context to provide an indication of a given country's 'trade activity'.

CO₂ Emissions

CO₂ emissions measured in kilotonnes. CO₂ emissions are the most commonly used mean to measure the environment impact of a country alongside temperature increases.


The filters are provided to see where Continents and different sized populations rank against eachother when comparing CO₂ emissions produced.

How do these relate?

From the visual, we can see a positive correlation between GDP per capita and CO₂ emissions produced. This may be a result of more trade activity. More productivity in a country means that there is more import/export transportand more industrial work proceeding.

Filtering by continent shows that South America is the Continent with the least CO₂ emissions per country. Interestingly, the forestation map above shows South America is very green, indicating there may be some correlation between more land forestation and less Carbon Dioxide Emissions.

Contrary to popular belief, countries in Asia do not produce the most Carbon Dioxide, but mainly countries within North America and Europe contribute the most.




Temperature Rise Over Time


Global Warming

We may have heard of the term 'Global 'Warming', but what does this phrase actually mean? It is a noun reserved to describe the slowly, but steadily increasing temperature across the world. This phenomena comes to fruition when analysing the rising water level as a result of the melting ice caps.

Temperature Change

The temperature change used in the visual is the net change in temperature by continent represented by decade.

Why?

It is clear that in general, the average temperature for each continent rises over time. This is due to the Ozone layer being destroyed over time.

Ozone Layer

The Ozone Layer in laymans terms is essentially an invisible layer that products the Earth from the full force of the Sun. Temperature change can be classified by continent as temperature change will change depending on the Ozone layer degradation in that region.




Agricultural and Urban Land


Agricultural Land

Agricultural land is measured as a percentage of total land. We can deduce that a country which is partially self-sustainable will have more agricultural land. It could also mean that the country is less developed.

Urban Land

Urban land is measured as a percentage of total land as well. A country that is 'urban' can be assumed to be more developed and industrial. As a result, a more 'urban' country may be classified as this due to high production levels requiring more space to expand.

'Scroll' on the visual to zoom in.

The trend

From the graph, an inversely proportional relationship between the two measures seems to be present. This may be a result of a country naturally or artificially focusing on the growth of one notion or the other.

Relation to Emissions?

Regardless of which type of land countries are more abundant of, CO₂ emissions seem to be mostly signifcant when a country is severely lacking in one or the other. Therefore, a balance is requried to ensure a country is not over-producing or over-importing, both which will increase artificial CO₂ emissions.
